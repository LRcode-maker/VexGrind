03 Standard VEX V5 Match Format

While we're waiting on Override's specific rules, the core VEX V5 competition structure stays consistent season to season. Here's the match format every team needs to understand going into 2026–27:

🏆
12' × 12' Field
The standard V5RC playing field, used for all head-to-head matches
Standard VEX V5 competition structure — consistent across seasons, reliable for planning your build strategy early.

Autonomous Period

The first 15 seconds of every match are fully autonomous — robots run pre-programmed routines with no driver input. This phase is critically important: the alliance that scores more points during autonomous receives a bonus added to their final score. Each alliance can also separately earn an Autonomous Win Point (AWP) by completing a set of assigned tasks before the buzzer. Both alliances can earn the AWP regardless of who wins the autonomous bonus.

Driver Controlled Period

After autonomous, drivers take the wheel for 1 minute and 45 seconds of head-to-head gameplay. This is where strategy, driver skill, and robot reliability become the deciding factors. Teams also compete in Robot Skills matches throughout the season — solo runs where one robot tries to score as many points as possible, combining a Driving Skills score and an Autonomous Coding Skills score.

  • Qualification Matches →Teams compete in multiple rounds to build Win Points (WP), Autonomous Win Points (AWP), and Strength of Schedule rankings.
  • Alliance Selection →Top-ranked teams pick their alliance partners for elimination rounds — one of the most strategic moments of any tournament.
  • Elimination Rounds →Best-of-3 matches between alliances, culminating in the finals. One alliance takes home the trophy.
  • Robot Skills Challenge →Separate scoring ladder running all season. High skills scores can earn World Championship invitations independent of match results.

What the Name "Override" Hints At

VEX game names have historically been descriptive clues to their mechanics — Over Under had robots going under barriers and over them, High Stakes involved staking rings at height, Push Back featured robots literally pushing blocks back. The name Override suggests themes of overriding an opponent's control, flipping scoring states, or some mechanic where robots can reverse or negate what the other alliance has scored. It could point to a "capture the flag" style control element, or a mechanic where scoring positions can be taken over by either side.

🔭 Community Theories

The name "Override" has sparked enormous speculation — switches that can be flipped, zones that change ownership, game objects that can be "overridden" by opponents. The reveal video is being combed for every visual clue. We'll have a full breakdown once the manual drops Monday.
